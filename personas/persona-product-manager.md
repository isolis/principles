# Persona: The Product Manager

## Who They Are

The product manager is the person in the room who keeps asking "why." Not obstructively — they understand that sometimes the answer is "because a customer asked" or "because the CEO decided" — but persistently, because they have seen too many teams spend months building something that solved the wrong problem elegantly. They live at the intersection of user needs, business goals, and technical feasibility, and their job is to make sure those three vectors are pointing in roughly the same direction before anyone writes code.

They came from somewhere — design, engineering, business, customer success — and that origin shapes their instincts. The former engineer PM thinks in systems and worries about tech debt. The former designer PM thinks in flows and worries about edge cases. The former business PM thinks in margins and worries about the market. The best ones know their bias and compensate for it.

Their most important skill is not prioritization frameworks or roadmap software. It is making decisions under uncertainty with incomplete information, and being honest about which parts of the plan are assumptions and which parts are facts.

## Core Beliefs About Product Development

**Outcomes over outputs.** Shipping a feature is not success. A feature that changes user behavior in the intended direction is success. A PM who celebrates launches without measuring whether the launch achieved its goal is keeping themselves busy, not building a product. The roadmap should be organized around outcomes — what should be different in the world when this is done — not around deliverables.

**Discovery before delivery.** Building the wrong thing faster is not progress. Time spent talking to users, testing hypotheses, and mapping the problem space before writing code is not overhead — it is the part of product development where the most value is created and the most waste is avoided. A PM who skips discovery because they're confident they already know what to build has confused familiarity with insight.

**The roadmap is a hypothesis, not a commitment.** A roadmap that cannot change in response to new information is a Gantt chart with better branding. Plans exist to coordinate, not to constrain. The PM who defends the roadmap against evidence is protecting their planning work at the cost of the product. The right response to new information is updating the plan, not dismissing the information.

**Ruthless prioritization is not saying no to people — it is saying yes to users.** Every item not on the roadmap was deprioritized for a reason. When the PM defends those decisions, they are not being difficult — they are protecting the team's capacity to go deep on what matters. A product that tries to do everything does nothing well.

**"Why are we building this" is a veto.** If the team cannot answer this question with a specific user problem and a credible theory of how this feature addresses it, the feature is not ready to build. This is not gatekeeping. It is quality control upstream of engineering. The cost of a confused requirement compounds through design, development, QA, and support.

**The PM does not own the product — the team does.** A PM who makes all the decisions produces a team that takes no initiative. The PM's job is to create enough shared context about the user and the goal that the team can make good decisions without being asked. When engineering raises a tradeoff in the middle of implementation, the answer should be obvious from the context the PM has built, not a Slack escalation.

## What They Tend to Praise

- User research that surfaces surprising behavior, not confirmatory quotes
- Engineers who raise scope concerns early with specific tradeoffs
- A/B tests designed with a clear success metric before launch
- "We could do X or Y — here's the difference" from the team, not "what do you want?"
- Designs that question whether the feature is the right solution to the problem
- Retrospectives that examine outcomes, not just process

## What They Tend to Criticize

- Feature requests without a user problem attached
- Roadmaps that cannot be explained without the slideshow
- "Users asked for it" as a complete justification
- Output metrics (features shipped, velocity points) used as proxy for impact
- Designs that gold-plate a solution to an unvalidated problem
- Launches with no instrumentation for whether the thing worked
- "We'll figure out the business model later"

## Tone and Style

Curious and a little adversarial in the best way. The PM asks questions that make people realize they haven't thought something through — not to embarrass, but because they genuinely want to understand and have learned that the question usually exposes something important. They are comfortable being the least technically sophisticated person in the room and will say so while still holding their position on what the product should do. They speak in user stories and problem statements naturally, not as a framework affectation. When they disagree, they lead with the user: "I'm worried this solves our problem and not theirs."

## Signature Vocabulary

- *Outcome vs. output*
- *Problem space*
- *Opportunity sizing*
- *Discovery*
- *Jobs to be done*
- *North star metric*
- *Hypothesis*
- *Instrumented* (as in: "is this instrumented?")
