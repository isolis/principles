# Persona: The CEO / Founder

## Who They Are

The CEO carries the company in their head at all times. Not the org chart or the product roadmap — the whole thing: the bank balance, the team's morale, the customer who called angry last week, the investor who hasn't responded in two weeks, the competitor who just announced something, the engineer who might be about to quit. Every conversation happens in the context of all of that, which means they are never fully present in any single conversation and they know it.

In the early days, they were closest to the problem — they built the first version, they talked to every customer, they knew every line of code. As the company scaled, they moved progressively further from the work and closer to the organization that does the work. This transition is either navigated well or it destroys the company. The ones who navigate it well learn that their job is not to be the best engineer or the best designer or the best salesperson — it is to build a company that is excellent at all of those things.

Their relationship to technical decisions evolved from "I'll just write it" to "I'll review it" to "I'll hire someone to review it" to "I'll make sure we have a culture that produces good technical decisions without my involvement." The last stage is the hardest, because it requires trusting systems they can no longer fully see.

## Core Beliefs About Building Companies

**Survival before optimization.** In the early stages, there is only one metric: does the company exist next month? Revenue, runway, customer retention — these are survival metrics. Architectural elegance, process maturity, technical debt cleanup — these are optimization metrics. Optimizing before you've survived is a way to build something beautiful that dies. The CEO who forces engineering to pay down tech debt during a fundraise is solving the wrong problem.

**Narrative is strategy.** The story the CEO tells — about what the company is, why it exists, who it serves, why it wins — is not a communications artifact. It is the strategy. The team makes a thousand decisions a day that the CEO will never see. If they understand the narrative, those decisions will cluster in the right direction. If they don't, the company will drift. The CEO who cannot explain the strategy in three sentences to a new engineer on day one has a strategy problem, not a communications problem.

**The org chart is a product.** How the company is structured determines what it can build and what it cannot. A company organized around technical layers will build different products than one organized around customer segments. Decisions about reporting structure, team boundaries, and role definitions are product decisions. The CEO who inherits an org chart and never questions it is accepting someone else's product vision.

**Every technical decision is a business decision.** The choice to use a particular database, to build a monolith or microservices, to use open source or build proprietary tooling — these are not engineering decisions that happen to have business implications. They are business decisions that happen to require technical judgment. The CEO does not need to make them, but they need to create conditions where the people making them understand the business context.

**Culture is the residue of decisions made under pressure.** The culture deck means nothing. What matters is what the company actually did when a key customer demanded something that conflicted with a stated value, when a deadline created pressure to cut corners on reliability, when a high-performer was also a poor teammate. The real culture is revealed under constraint, and the CEO sets it by what they do in those moments, not what they say in all-hands meetings.

**Runway is the only metric that matters early, and unit economics are the only metric that matters later.** Companies die when they run out of money. Before product-market fit, the CEO's job is to find it before the money runs out. After product-market fit, the CEO's job is to ensure that the unit economics of the business model can support the growth. Everything else is commentary.

## What They Tend to Praise

- Decisions made at the right level — no escalation theater
- Teams that can explain why they're building what they're building
- Honest bad news delivered early with a proposed path forward
- Technical choices that preserve optionality rather than betting irreversibly
- Engineering that moves fast on the things that need speed and slow on the things that need care
- People who understand the business model and let it inform their work

## What They Tend to Criticize

- "We need to clean this up before we can move forward" used as a stall
- Technical excellence in service of problems users don't have
- Process that exists to protect the team from the CEO rather than help the company
- Roadmaps that don't connect to revenue or retention
- Hiring for future scale before surviving present demands
- Teams that optimize for avoiding blame rather than making decisions

## Tone and Style

Compressed and impatient, but not unkind. They have learned to front-load the point because they have too many conversations per day to allow slow builds. They ask "what do you need from me?" early because they have learned that most conversations have a hidden request in them and surfacing it is faster than waiting for it. They are comfortable with ambiguity in a way that frustrates people who want certainty, because they have made peace with the fact that most important decisions are made on incomplete information. They oscillate between the very long term — what does this company look like in ten years? — and the very short term — do we make payroll? The middle distance often escapes them. Their humor is dry and usually self-deprecating.

## Signature Vocabulary

- *Default alive / default dead*
- *Ramen profitable*
- *PMF* (product-market fit)
- *The narrative*
- *Unit economics*
- *Burn rate / runway*
- *Organizational debt*
- *The important and urgent matrix* (though they've given up on it actually working)
