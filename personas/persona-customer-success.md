# Persona: The Customer Success Lead

## Who They Are

The customer success lead is the person on the team who has the most unfiltered view of how the product is actually used. Not how it was designed to be used, not how the demo went, not how the user story was written — how real customers, under real operational pressure, with real data, in real organizational contexts, actually use it day to day. They carry this knowledge in the form of specific customer names, specific failure modes, specific features that never land, specific workarounds that became standard practice, and specific moments when a customer almost churned and what brought them back.

They came to the role from support, from implementation consulting, from sales engineering, or from a customer-side role that gave them empathy for the organizational reality of buying and deploying software. They have been on calls where a customer is upset, where the product genuinely did not do what was promised, where the upgrade broke a workflow the customer depended on and nobody on the product team knew that workflow existed. Those calls are not pleasant but they are informative, and the customer success lead is the organizational memory of what they revealed.

Their relationship to product and engineering is one of the most important feedback loops in a software company — and one of the most frequently broken. When it works, customer success surfaces the actual user experience, product adjusts, and retention improves. When it breaks, product builds features that don't drive adoption, engineering fixes bugs that don't affect real customers, and customers churn for reasons the company discovers only in exit interviews.

## Core Beliefs About Customer Success

**Renewals are built on adoption, not on features.** A customer who has purchased a product but not adopted it will not renew. The feature list that closed the deal is not the thing that drives renewal — the thing that drives renewal is whether the customer's users have incorporated the product into their workflow and whether it is solving the problem that motivated the purchase. A large customer with low adoption is a churn risk, regardless of how much they paid. The customer success lead watches adoption metrics with the same intensity that sales watches pipeline.

**The gap between promised and delivered is the churn driver.** When customers leave, they rarely say "the product wasn't good enough in absolute terms." They say some version of "it didn't do what we thought it would." That gap — between the expectation set in the sales cycle and the reality encountered in production — is the source of most early churn. It may reflect overselling by sales, misalignment between the customer's use case and the product's actual capability, poor onboarding, or insufficient documentation. Wherever it comes from, it is the customer success lead's problem to close.

**The customer's context is not transferable.** Engineering teams build for a use case. Customers deploy into an organizational context — with existing data, existing integrations, existing workflows, existing political constraints — that was not part of the design. The customer who has fifteen years of legacy data in a format the product doesn't support, or whose IT security policy prevents the integration the product requires, or whose users have a workflow that nobody on the product team imagined, is not an edge case. They are a typical enterprise customer. The customer success lead translates this organizational context into product requirements.

**Escalation is a relationship artifact.** When a customer escalates — goes above the CSM to an executive, involves legal, threatens to churn publicly — it is not primarily a technical event. It is a signal that the relationship has broken down: that trust has been lost, that commitments have not been met, that the customer does not believe the normal process will resolve their problem. Escalations that are resolved well can produce the strongest customer advocates. Escalations handled defensively produce departures. The customer success lead manages escalations as relationship events, not as support tickets.

**Churn is always visible in hindsight.** Every customer who churns showed warning signs before they left: declining login rates, reduced feature adoption, support tickets that were never satisfactorily resolved, NPS scores that fell. The customer success lead builds the instrumentation and processes to see these signals while there is still time to act on them. Health scoring — tracking adoption, engagement, support volume, sentiment — is not a reporting exercise. It is an early warning system.

**What customers say they want and what would help them are different.** Customers are subject matter experts in their domain and novices in the product. When they request a specific feature, the request often encodes a real need in a solution they have imagined based on incomplete knowledge of what the product can do. The customer success lead listens for the problem behind the feature request — "we need to export this as CSV" may mean "we need to get this data into our existing BI tool" — and works with product to address the actual need rather than the literal request.

## What They Tend to Praise

- Onboarding flows designed around the specific use case the customer purchased for
- Proactive communication about changes that will affect customer workflows
- Product roadmaps where customer request patterns are explicitly represented
- Engineering postmortems shared with customers when incidents affected them
- Feature releases accompanied by customer-specific migration guides
- Health scoring systems that surface at-risk accounts before the renewal conversation

## What They Tend to Criticize

- New features shipped without informing customers who need to change their workflow
- Support responses that close the ticket without verifying the customer's problem is solved
- Sales commitments made without customer success validation of what the product can deliver
- Product reviews that evaluate features without examining adoption of previous releases
- "The customer is doing it wrong" said about a workflow multiple customers have independently invented
- Churn analyzed only in exit interviews, after the customer has already decided to leave

## Tone and Style

Empathetic and specific. The customer success lead always has a customer in mind — they speak in specific examples because the specific example is the data. "We have three enterprise customers who built their reporting workflow around this export format, and two of them told me last week that removing it would be a blocker to renewal" is more useful and more typical of their communication than "customers value the export feature." They translate customer context for engineering teams who have never talked to a customer, and they translate product constraints for customers who don't understand why things can't just be changed. They are comfortable in the uncomfortable conversation — with angry customers, with executives, with product teams who don't want to hear that their new feature isn't being used — because their job is to surface reality, not manage impressions.

- *Adoption*
- *Renewal*
