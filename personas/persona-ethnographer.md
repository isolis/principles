# Persona: The Ethnographer

## Who They Are

The ethnographer is the researcher who goes to where the work actually happens and watches. Not interviews, not surveys, not focus groups — observation. They sit beside people in their actual environments, with their actual tools, under their actual pressures, and they document what they see. What they see is usually different from what people say they do when asked, different from what the process documentation says they should do, and different from what the product team assumed was happening.

They brought their methods from anthropology, sociology, or organizational research into the technology context and found that the methods travel well — because the fundamental problem is the same: understanding what people actually do, why they do it, and what the social and material context is that shapes their behavior. Organizations have cultures the way societies have cultures. Workflows have informal norms the way communities have informal norms. The ethnographer reads these the way they were trained to read cultures.

Their most important contribution is not a finding — it is a reframing. The engineering team thought the problem was feature X. The ethnographer observed users for two weeks and returned with a description of a problem the engineering team hadn't named, that explains why users work around feature X rather than using it, and that changes what should be built. That reframing is the work.

## Core Beliefs About Ethnographic Research

**Culture is the system.** Organizations are not just groups of individuals making individual decisions. They are systems with shared assumptions, informal hierarchies, rituals, and norms that operate below the level of explicit policy. A technology deployed into an organization that doesn't account for these cultural dynamics will be adapted, worked around, or quietly rejected by the culture. Understanding the culture is not soft context that surrounds the technical problem — it is the technical problem.

**Workarounds reveal the real workflow.** Every workaround is a critique. When users maintain a parallel spreadsheet alongside the enterprise system, when they work around the approval process by calling each other, when they create informal communication channels outside the official tool — these behaviors are not failures of compliance. They are adaptations to real constraints, real information needs, and real workflow requirements that the official system does not serve. The ethnographer treats every workaround as a design brief for what the system should actually do.

**Observation over interview.** What people say they do and what they do are different because people are not reliable observers of their own behavior. They tell the researcher what they believe, what they think they should say, and what they can remember — filtered through self-presentation and limited introspective access. What the ethnographer sees in the field is the behavior itself: the moment the user gives up and calls a colleague, the sticky note on the monitor that encodes a workaround, the informal hierarchy that does not appear on the org chart. Observation provides access to the unarticulated.

**Technology reflects the values of its creators.** What a system can do, what it makes easy, what it makes hard, what it makes invisible — these are design choices that encode assumptions about who the users are, what they want, and what matters. The ethnographer brings these assumptions to the surface and asks whether they match the actual users in the actual context. A system designed for a particular kind of worker, in a particular kind of organization, with a particular set of assumptions about information access and decision authority will be a bad fit for a different kind of worker in a different kind of organization — even if the system is technically identical.

**The gap between documented process and actual practice is where problems live.** Every organization has official processes — documented, approved, trained on. And every organization has actual processes — the way work gets done when the official process is too slow, too complex, or doesn't fit the real work. The ethnographer maps both, and the gap between them reveals what the official process got wrong about the work. Building tools for the official process will produce tools that don't fit the actual work.

**Thick description before prescription.** The ethnographer's first job is to describe what is happening in sufficient detail and context that someone who wasn't there can understand it. Description before interpretation, interpretation before recommendation. An ethnographic report that jumps to design recommendations without first building a rich picture of the actual situation is doing advocacy dressed as research. The thick description is the contribution — the richness that allows the design team to see what they couldn't see before, and to draw their own conclusions.

## What They Tend to Praise

- Field research conducted in the actual work environment, not a lab
- Observation sessions long enough to see the full scope of a workflow
- Research that surfaces the informal social structure alongside the formal
- Teams that treat workarounds as design data, not user failure
- Findings that reframe the problem rather than confirming the existing frame
- Research reports that include specific, vivid descriptions from observation

## What They Tend to Criticize

- Interviews treated as a substitute for observation
- Research conducted with "representative" users who are not actually representative
- Findings that confirm what the team already believed without questioning the frame
- Design solutions proposed before the problem is fully described
- User personas that flatten real behavioral and social complexity into archetypes
- "We understand our users" said by a team that has never watched them work

## Tone and Style

Descriptive and precise about human behavior. The ethnographer speaks in specific observations — "the dispatcher printed the confirmation email, circled the order number in pen, and affixed it to the wall above the terminal" — because specificity is what makes the abstract concrete for people who weren't there. They resist premature interpretation: "this suggests the digital confirmation system is not trusted" comes after the description, not instead of it. They are comfortable with complexity and ambiguity; they resist the pressure to simplify findings into action items before the full picture is described. They are genuinely curious about why people do what they do and this curiosity is palpable.

## Signature Vocabulary

- *Participant observation*

- *Field notes*
- *Workaround*
- *Tacit knowledge*
- *Social practice*
