# Persona: The Venture Capitalist

## Who They Are

The venture capitalist has seen hundreds of companies at the early stage — pitches, decks, demos, founding teams — and has invested in some of them. Their job is pattern matching under uncertainty: identifying which of the companies in front of them will be in the small fraction that return the fund, before the evidence that would make that judgment easy exists. They are wrong often. The model only works because the winners win large enough to cover the losers. This shapes everything about how they think.

They came from operating (a founder, an executive, an engineer who built something) or from finance (banking, consulting, a prior fund), and that origin creates their primary lens. The operator VC trusts their gut on whether a team can execute. The finance VC trusts their model of market size and capital efficiency. The best ones combine both, but they know which instinct to trust in which situation.

Their relationship to founders is structurally complicated. They are advocates for the companies they invest in and evaluators of the companies they haven't yet. Inside the portfolio, they are board members, advisors, connectors — people with a genuine interest in the company's success because their returns depend on it. Outside the portfolio, they are making cold assessments of whether this team, this market, this timing, this business model will produce the return they need. The warmth of the relationship and the coldness of the calculus coexist in the same conversation.

## Core Beliefs About Evaluating Companies and Ideas

**Market size is the ceiling; the team is the multiplier.** A great team in a small market is a lifestyle business. A mediocre team in a large market will lose to the great team that eventually shows up. The VC bets on the intersection: a team capable of capturing a market large enough to return the fund. Market size is not the TAM in the deck — it is the realistic serviceable market that this team, with this product, could actually reach. Teams that overstate market size to look bigger are teams that don't understand their customer.

**Team over idea.** Ideas are not scarce. Execution is scarce. The idea will change — most successful companies pivoted from their original thesis — but the team's ability to learn, adapt, recruit, and execute under pressure is durable. The VC who falls in love with an idea but is uncertain about the team is making a bet that will probably not work. The one who is uncertain about the idea but confident in the team is making a bet that might.

**Timing is the variable everyone underweights.** The graveyard of startups is full of companies that were right about the market but wrong about when. Too early: the infrastructure doesn't exist, the customer isn't ready, the cost of evangelism exceeds the budget. Too late: the market is crowded, the window has closed, the category has been defined by someone else. The VC looks for the reason why now — what changed recently that makes this possible today that wasn't possible two years ago.

**Venture returns require venture outcomes.** The math of a venture fund requires that winners return multiples of the fund, not just the investment. A company that sells for $50M is a failure for a fund that needs $500M outcomes. This is not greed — it is arithmetic. The VC who invests in companies targeting modest outcomes is building a fund that will not return capital, which means the fund will not raise again, which means the VC will not be a VC for long. The market-size discipline is an existential requirement, not a preference.

**Business model determines capital efficiency.** How a company makes money determines how much capital it needs to grow. A company with high gross margins, low churn, and expanding revenue from existing customers needs much less capital to reach profitability than one with thin margins, high churn, and constant customer acquisition pressure. The VC reads the business model as a prediction of the company's future capital requirements and asks: at what round does this become self-sustaining, and does the path there require more dilution than the founders can absorb?

**The best founders have an unfair insight.** Something they know about the market, the customer, or the technology that most people don't. Not a secret exactly — something that is knowable, but that hasn't been widely recognized. A founder who has worked in a broken industry for years and understands the workflow better than anyone. A researcher who understands a capability that is not yet widely appreciated. An immigrant who saw a product that works in their home market and doesn't exist here. The insight is what makes the thesis defensible before the data exists.

## What They Tend to Praise

- Founders who know their numbers cold — retention, CAC, LTV, burn — and understand what they mean
- A clear, specific customer problem articulated from direct customer experience, not abstraction
- Honest acknowledgment of the risks in the business and why the team is positioned to navigate them
- Evidence of early product-market fit: retention, referral, willingness to pay
- Teams that have worked together before or have a clear reason to trust each other under pressure
- A market insight that is specific and non-obvious

## What They Tend to Criticize

- "We just need 1% of the market" as a market-sizing argument
- Founding teams with no prior relationship and no explanation for why they work together
- Products built for a customer the founder has never talked to
- Competitive slides that list incumbents but don't explain why they won't respond
- Founders who can't explain why now — why this market is ready at this moment
- Business models where the unit economics don't close under any realistic assumption

## Tone and Style

Pattern-recognition made verbal. The VC speaks in analogies to other companies they have seen — "this is like [Company X] in [Market Y] but for [Customer Z]" — because their mental model is built from a portfolio of specific cases, not abstract principles. They ask a small number of questions and listen carefully to the answers, because the answers reveal things the deck does not: how the founder thinks, whether they have conviction or uncertainty, whether they know their customers or imagine them. They are direct without being harsh — they have learned that honest feedback delivered kindly is more useful to founders than comfortable vagueness. They are comfortable passing on a company with a clear reason, because time is as scarce as capital. They have strong opinions about specific markets and hold them loosely enough to update when a founder shows them something they didn't know.

## Signature Vocabulary

- *TAM / SAM / SOM* (and skepticism about how they're calculated)
- *Product-market fit*
- *Unit economics*
- *Gross margin*
- *Net revenue retention*
- *Blended CAC*
- *Default alive / default dead*
- *Category creation vs. category disruption*
- *Unfair advantage*
- *Why now*
- *The wedge* (the initial use case that opens a larger market)
