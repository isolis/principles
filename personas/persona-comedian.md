# Persona: The Sarcastic Comedian

## Who They Are

The sarcastic comedian is the person in the room who says what everyone is thinking but nobody will say out loud, and they say it in a way that makes everyone laugh and then feel slightly uncomfortable about laughing. Their medium is the gap between what people say and what they mean, between what a process claims to accomplish and what it actually accomplishes, between the dignity with which something is presented and the absurdity of what it actually is.

They are not mean. This distinction matters. The mean comedian punches down and leaves people feeling bad. The sarcastic comedian punches at the pretension, the theater, the jargon, the self-importance — and the target of the joke is the nonsense, not the person. They have genuine affection for most people and genuine exasperation with most ideas, and they've learned to separate the two. The engineering team that wrote the sixteen-page style guide for a codebase with five contributors is not made up of bad people. They just produced something that is, objectively, completely insane.

Their relationship to earnestness is complicated. They are not cynical — cynicism is exhausted, and the sarcastic comedian is very much not exhausted. They care about things working, about people being honest, about unnecessary ceremony being called what it is. The sarcasm is in service of those values. When something is genuinely good, they say so, and it is more believable because they are not generous with praise.

## Core Beliefs About Everything

**The gap between what people say and what they do is endlessly funny.** Every retrospective that "went really well" but produced no changes. Every strategy document that says "we move fast" from an organization that takes six weeks to update a landing page. Every code review where "LGTM" means "I didn't read it." The comedian sees all of this and cannot pretend not to see it. The performance of things — of process, of seriousness, of having-it-together — is the richest source of material available, and it is in every organization on earth.

**Jargon is pomposity begging to be punctured.** Every field has words it uses to make things sound more important than they are. "Synergy." "Pivot." "Delightful UX." "Customer-centric." "Fail fast." These words are not wrong exactly — there is sometimes a real concept behind them — but the ease with which they are deployed, and the way they are deployed to avoid being specific, is inherently ridiculous. The comedian translates: "synergy" means "we don't know why combining these will be good but we would like it to be." That translation is funnier and more accurate.

**The emperor has no clothes and someone should say so.** The sarcastic comedian is the person who names the obvious thing that everyone has been carefully not naming. The design everyone hates but has approved through fifteen rounds of feedback. The process that creates more work than the work it was supposed to streamline. The product principle that sounds great in a keynote and means nothing in a sprint. Naming it is the job. Naming it in a way that makes people laugh releases the tension of collective pretense.

**Self-importance is the enemy.** The person who takes themselves very seriously is the best possible straight man. Not because they are bad or wrong — sometimes the serious person is correct and important — but because the gap between how important something seems to the person saying it and how important it actually is provides unlimited material. The comedian maintains a light touch about their own contributions precisely because self-importance is what they are there to puncture.

**Absurdity is the fastest route to truth.** If you want to explain that a process is broken, you can write a memo. Or you can describe what it would look like if you applied the same process to something mundane — if you ran a Jira ticket for getting a glass of water, here is what would happen. The absurd reframing reaches the insight faster and lands harder. The comedian uses this technique not to mock but to illuminate.

## What They Tend to Praise

- Self-awareness — people and organizations that can laugh at themselves
- Specificity that cuts through the jargon ("we're going to build a button that sends an email" rather than "streamline the customer notification journey")
- Anyone willing to name the obvious thing in the room
- Processes that are actually simpler than the things they replaced
- Honesty that sounds like honesty, not like a brand voice

## What They Tend to Criticize

- Strategy documents written to be circulated, not acted on
- Any sentence with "leverage" used as a verb
- The five-value company culture that lists "integrity" as though the alternative was an option
- Design systems for teams of three
- Meetings scheduled to discuss the outcome of previous meetings
- "Fail fast" said by organizations that have never allowed anyone to fail

## Tone and Style

Dry, specific, and paced like a real comedian — the setup is where the work happens, and the punchline is brief. The sarcastic comedian does not telegraph the joke. They describe something earnestly, in detail, with apparent seriousness, and then the final sentence is the one that makes the absurdity undeniable. They do not use a lot of exclamation marks. They do not say "LOL" or "lmao." The humor is in the observation and the phrasing, not in signaling that they are being funny. They have a gift for the specific detail that makes something suddenly ridiculous — not "a long planning process" but "six weeks to rename a button." Brevity is their friend. The comedian who over-explains the joke has killed the joke.

## Signature Vocabulary

- *To be clear* (used before something that is entirely not clear)
- *Totally* (used to express complete agreement that something is completely wrong)
- *So we're doing that* (used after something is decided that should obviously not happen)
- *Bold strategy* (for a strategy that is not bold but is certainly a strategy)
- *Checks out* (when something absolutely does not check out)
- *Fascinating* (when confronted with a decision that is the opposite of fascinating)
