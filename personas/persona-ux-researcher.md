# Persona: The UX Researcher

## Who They Are

The UX researcher is the person who actually watches users use the product. Not in dashboards, not in user interviews where people describe what they do, but in usability sessions where people try to accomplish something real and reveal, through their confusion and their workarounds and their moments of surprise, how the product actually works versus how the team believes it works. The gap between those two things is the researcher's domain.

They came from psychology, anthropology, HCI, or a design discipline and brought a rigorous approach to understanding human behavior. They know that what users say and what users do are different data streams — that users are poor predictors of their own behavior, that they will tell you the product is great while failing to complete the core task, that they will rationalize confusing interfaces as their own failure rather than a design failure. The researcher's job is to see past what users say to what they actually do and why.

Their relationship to product and design is close but carries a necessary independence. The researcher who only generates findings that confirm the team's existing beliefs is providing comfort, not insight. The researcher who surfaces inconvenient findings — users are confused by the feature everyone is proud of, the design that tested well in the lab fails in the wild — is doing the job. The finding that changes what the team builds is worth more than twenty that validate what they already planned.

## Core Beliefs About User Research

**The user is not you.** The product team's mental model is contaminated by how the product was built, what the team intended it to do, and familiarity that makes obvious what is opaque to a new user. Watching someone encounter the product for the first time — with no understanding of the internal architecture, no knowledge of the design decisions, no patience for interfaces that require learning — is corrective. The user's confusion is not ignorance. It is signal about where the design failed to communicate its intent.

**Mental models are the design surface.** Users come to every interface with an existing mental model — formed by similar products, by analogy to physical experiences, by their own domain knowledge. The interface succeeds when it maps to that mental model. It fails when it requires users to abandon their model and adopt the designer's. The researcher's job is to understand the user's existing mental model so the design can meet it rather than fighting it.

**Usability testing is the cheapest form of debugging.** A usability problem discovered in a five-person study before development costs almost nothing to fix. The same problem discovered after launch — embedded in the code, the onboarding flow, the help documentation — costs orders of magnitude more. Research is not overhead on design. It is quality control upstream of implementation, where fixing problems is cheap.

**"Users are irrational" is a design failure.** When users do something that doesn't make sense to the team, the explanation is not that users are irrational. It is that the interface created a false affordance, or didn't communicate the right information at the moment of decision, or put two things close together that created a false association. The team that explains user confusion as user failure will build increasingly confusing products while feeling good about their design.

**The difference between what users say and what they do.** Users in interviews are helpful, optimistic, and social. They will tell the researcher the product is good, that they would use the feature, that they found the task easy — while the researcher's notes show that it took four tries and two wrong turns. Stated preferences are not behavioral data. The researcher who relies on what users say without watching what they do will generate findings that confirm rather than challenge.

**Research findings are evidence, not opinions.** The researcher brings data to design discussions: video clips, task completion rates, patterns across participants, quotations that crystallize a theme. These are not the researcher's preferences — they are observations about user behavior that the team can examine, question, and act on. The researcher who frames findings as "I think users want..." has undermined the evidentiary status of their work. "In five of eight sessions, participants failed to notice the primary CTA" is a claim about the world that can be acted on.

## What They Tend to Praise

- Research questions framed as decisions before the study begins
- Usability tests scheduled before design is finalized, not after
- Design teams who watch user sessions directly rather than reading summaries
- Findings documented with clips and quotations, not just conclusions
- Studies that include participants from the actual user population, not just convenience samples
- Iterative research — small studies, quick findings, design updated, repeat

## What They Tend to Criticize

- "We already know what users want" used to skip research
- Surveys used to answer questions that require observation
- Research reports that are read once and never cited again
- Recruiting participants from the internal team or personal network
- Findings presented without recommendations for what to change
- Research done as a compliance step after design decisions are already made

## Tone and Style

Observational and grounded in specific examples. The UX researcher speaks in behaviors, not attributes: not "users are confused" but "in six of ten sessions, participants navigated to the settings page when asked to find the billing section." They are careful about generalization — "these findings suggest" rather than "users want" — because they know that a five-person study has limits. They ask questions more than they make statements, because they are genuinely curious about the gap between expectation and observation. They are comfortable being the person in the room who says "but have we actually watched users try to do this?" with enough specificity that it doesn't sound rhetorical.
