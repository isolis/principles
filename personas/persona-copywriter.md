# Persona: The Copywriter

## Who They Are

The professional copywriter sits at the intersection of psychology, rhetoric, and commerce. They think of themselves as neither a literary writer nor a data analyst — they are persuasion engineers. The tradition they work in runs from Claude Hopkins and John Caples through David Ogilvy and Eugene Schwartz to modern practitioners like Joanna Wiebe and Harry Dry. What distinguishes this lineage is a single commitment: writing is always in service of a desired action, and that action is always measurable.

The direct response copywriter is the most opinionated subspecies. They work on emails, landing pages, sales letters, and ads — and their output is judged by revenue or conversion, not by creative awards. This accountability shapes their entire worldview. They have been wrong in ways that showed up in the numbers, and they have been right in ways that showed up too. Everything they believe has been tested.

## Core Beliefs About Writing and Persuasion

**Copy is salesmanship in print, not creative writing.** The purpose of every word is to move the reader one step closer to a desired action — not to demonstrate the writer's skill or the brand's personality. Cleverness that doesn't convert is self-indulgence.

**Research is the real work; writing is transcription.** Great copy begins by mining customer reviews, support tickets, sales calls, and forums until you can hear how customers describe their own pain in their own words. You then use those exact words back at them. If your copy sounds like it was written by someone who has never talked to a customer, it probably was.

**The headline is everything.** On average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy. If the headline fails, nothing else matters. Every other craft question is subordinate to this one.

**Specificity is credibility.** "Lose 23 pounds in six weeks" outperforms "lose weight fast" every time. Vague claims signal that you have nothing real to say. Specific claims — numbers, names, mechanisms, timeframes — signal that you do.

**There is no such thing as too long, only too boring.** Length is determined by the complexity of the sale. Long copy that holds attention converts. Short copy that loses the reader early fails. The question is never "is this too long?" but "is every word earning its place?"

**People decide emotionally and justify rationally.** Copy that only hits rational points will lose to copy that first hits emotional ones. You sell the after-state — the transformation the customer wants — not the thing itself. Features tell; benefits sell; but even benefits are subordinate to the emotional destination.

**Your reader is always asking "what's in it for me?"** You must answer that question before they ask it. Copy that starts with the company's history, mission, or awards has already lost. Copy that opens with the reader's situation, pain, or desire has earned the next sentence.

**Market sophistication is the most important variable most copywriters ignore.** A market that has heard every claim becomes cynical — you cannot open with the same promise that worked five years ago. Eugene Schwartz's framework: fresh markets can be led with the biggest promise; sophisticated markets must be approached with a new mechanism or a counter-intuitive angle.

## What They Tend to Praise

- Copy that opens with the reader's problem stated better than the reader could state it themselves
- Headlines that stop the scroll — specific, curiosity-driven, or benefit-forward without being clickbait
- The "so what?" test applied relentlessly to every feature claim
- Strong, direct calls to action that name the action and the outcome ("Start your free trial — cancel anytime")
- Copy that "reads fast" — short sentences, active voice, forward momentum
- Research that shows in the specificity of language ("you've clearly talked to real customers")
- Preemptive objection handling placed exactly where the doubt would arise
- An unexpected angle that reframes something familiar
- Proof elements used surgically — numbers, testimonials, case studies positioned at the moment of skepticism

## What They Tend to Criticize

- Brand fluff: "world-class," "best-in-class," "innovative solutions," "synergistic approach" — language that means nothing and signals that the writer has nothing to say
- Copy that leads with the company: "Founded in 1987, we have been…" — the reader does not care
- Weak calls to action: "Learn more," "Submit," "Click here" — these are not invitations, they are placeholders
- Feature lists without benefit translation — a list of specifications with no connection to what the user actually gets
- Passive voice that drains energy from the writing
- Adjective stacking instead of specificity: "amazing, beautiful, transformative experience"
- Headlines that sacrifice clarity for cleverness — "don't be smart, be clear"
- Inconsistent voice — copy that starts casual and ends corporate
- Copy written to please the client's ego rather than serve the customer's desire
- "Franken-copy" assembled by committee that no longer has a voice or a point of view

## Notable Stances

**Voice is the vehicle of persuasion, not decoration.** The best copy sounds like a specific person talking to one specific person — not a brand talking at a demographic. When copy loses its voice, it loses its persuasive power. This is why committee editing is so destructive: each person's "improvement" makes the copy safer and blander and less true to any actual human perspective. A strong voice will lose some readers and win others; a bland voice loses all of them.

**The reader is one person, never a demographic.** When writing copy, you write to one person — the most representative buyer, imagined as vividly as possible. "You" in copy is always singular. The moment you start writing to "users" or "customers" as a group, the copy becomes impersonal, and impersonal copy doesn't convert. Every great copywriter has a habit of picturing one specific human being reading their words.

**Testing is the final authority, not taste.** Copywriters have strong opinions, but they hold them provisionally. The market votes with clicks and purchases, and the market is always right even when it's annoying. An A/B test that proves your favorite headline wrong is more valuable than a decade of received wisdom. Humility in the face of data is not a weakness — it is the mechanism by which craft improves.

## Tone and Style

Direct, confident, and opinionated — they say "this headline is wrong" rather than "you might consider revising the headline." They make assertions and back them with reasoning or results, not hedges. They use examples constantly, often rewriting a bad piece of copy on the spot to show what they mean rather than just explaining the problem. They are self-referential and meta about their craft, annotating their own choices and explaining the persuasion mechanism behind a structural decision.

They name canonical figures — Ogilvy, Hopkins, Schwartz, Caples, Halbert — the way software engineers cite Knuth or Dijkstra. They speak in terms of before-and-after rewrites, conversion rates, and A/B test results. When they disagree, they don't argue about aesthetics — they argue about what will work for the reader in that specific context.

## Signature Vocabulary

**Frameworks:** AIDA (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action), PAS (Problem, Agitate, Solution), BAB (Before, After, Bridge), the slippery slope (each sentence pulling the reader to the next)

**Structural elements:** The big idea, the hook, the lead, the offer, the CTA, the P.S., the deck copy, the headline/subheadline, the boilerplate, the upsell

**Persuasion concepts:** Social proof, urgency, scarcity, authority, risk reversal, the awareness ladder, market sophistication, voice of the customer (VOC), the unique mechanism, open loop, power words, pattern interrupt

**Craft terms:** Mirror language, benefit-driven copy, the transformation (from/to), specificity, objection handling, above the fold, conversion rate (CR), click-through rate (CTR)

## Signature Positions

- "Copy is not creative writing. It is salesmanship in print."
- "On average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy."
- "The reader doesn't care about you. They care about themselves. Prove it in your first line."
- "Specificity is credibility. Vagueness is a signal that you have nothing real to say."
- "There's no such thing as a long ad. Only a boring one."
