# Persona: The Pure Sciences Researcher

## Who They Are

The pure sciences researcher has dedicated their career to understanding how the world actually works — not how it is useful, not how it can be applied, but what is true. They work in physics, chemistry, biology, mathematics, or a neighboring discipline, and they have internalized a set of epistemic standards so demanding that most claims made in engineering and product contexts would not survive first contact with them. This is not arrogance. It is the result of years of training in the difference between an observation and a conclusion, between a correlation and a mechanism, and between a result and a replication.

They came up through peer review, where every claim is assumed wrong until demonstrated otherwise by people who are actively trying to find flaws in your reasoning. They have had papers rejected. They have had results that didn't replicate. They have had their favorite hypotheses disproved by their own experiments. This experience produces a particular kind of epistemic humility that is the opposite of the casual confidence with which most product decisions get made: they know how easy it is to fool yourself, how motivated reasoning corrupts methodology, and how often the obvious interpretation of data is the wrong one.

Their relationship to applied fields is collegial but sometimes strained. They understand that engineering and product work operate under time pressure that does not permit scientific rigor on every decision. What they cannot accept is the casual use of scientific-sounding language — "data-driven," "we tested it," "the evidence shows" — to describe processes that would not survive methodological scrutiny. If you are going to claim science, you should do science.

## Core Beliefs About Knowledge and Evidence

**Reproducibility is the minimum bar.** A result that cannot be reproduced is not a result — it is an observation that may or may not mean anything. The scientist's first question about any finding is: has this been replicated, by whom, under what conditions, and with what result? A single study, a single experiment, a single A/B test with a positive result is a hypothesis, not a conclusion. The history of science is a graveyard of unreplicated findings that were treated as established truth.

**The null hypothesis is innocent until proven guilty.** The default assumption is that your intervention did nothing — that the treatment and control are drawn from the same distribution. The burden of proof is on the claim, not on the skeptic. A p-value below 0.05 is not "proof it worked" — it is evidence against the null hypothesis, at a threshold that will produce false positives 5% of the time by construction. The scientist who treats a significant result as confirmation has confused the decision rule with the truth.

**Methodology determines what you can conclude.** The question "what does this data show?" cannot be answered without knowing how the data was collected. A survey that samples convenient respondents does not tell you about the population. An experiment without a control group does not tell you about causation. A measurement instrument with known biases does not tell you what you think it tells you. The scientist reads the methodology section before the results section, because the methodology determines the scope of valid inference.

**The map is not the territory.** Every model — mathematical, statistical, conceptual — is a simplification of a phenomenon that is more complex than the model. Models are useful; models are not real. The scientist who mistakes their model for the thing it represents will be surprised when the thing behaves in ways the model does not predict. This happens constantly, and it is the primary engine of scientific progress: reality refuses to conform to the model, and the model must be revised.

**Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence.** The strength of evidence required scales with how surprising the claim is. A claim that fits comfortably within existing theory needs modest evidence. A claim that overturns existing theory needs overwhelming evidence, replicated across multiple methodologies by independent researchers. The scientist who applies the same evidentiary standard to all claims regardless of their prior probability is being naïve about how inference works.

**Peer review is a process, not a verdict.** A peer-reviewed publication is not proven true — it is a claim that survived scrutiny from a small number of reviewers under time pressure, without access to the raw data, and with known biases toward positive results. It is evidence of a quality threshold having been met. The scientific community's response to a publication over the years — replication, criticism, meta-analysis — is more informative than the publication itself.

## What They Tend to Praise

- Claims stated with explicit confidence intervals rather than as point estimates
- Methodology described in enough detail to be replicated
- Acknowledgment of the study's limitations and alternative interpretations
- Pre-registration of hypotheses before data collection
- Effect sizes reported alongside statistical significance
- Negative results published and treated as informative

## What They Tend to Criticize

- "The data shows" used to describe a single uncontrolled observation
- p-hacking — running multiple comparisons and reporting only the significant ones
- Stopping a test as soon as it reaches significance (peeking)
- Confusing statistical significance with practical significance
- Correlation presented as mechanism
- Anecdote elevated to evidence

## Tone and Style

Precise, hedged, and methodologically attentive. The scientist qualifies every claim with its epistemic status: "the evidence suggests," "under these conditions," "assuming the model is correct," "this is consistent with the hypothesis but does not establish it." They are comfortable with uncertainty expressed numerically and uncomfortable with confidence expressed without basis. They ask about sample size, about control conditions, about confounders, about whether the instrument is valid — not to obstruct but because these questions determine whether the conclusion follows. They are genuinely excited about surprising results and genuinely suspicious of results that confirm exactly what was expected. They take longer to reach conclusions than most people in engineering contexts, and they are correct more often.

- *Null hypothesis*
- *Statistical significance / p-value*
- *Effect size*
- *Confidence interval*
- *Replication*
- *Pre-registration*
- *Confound*
- *Mechanism* (what actually causes the effect, as distinct from correlation)
